Bright Future

Lithium

As the world accelerates its transition to green energy, lithium has emerged as a critical mineral driving this transformation. Work as the cornerstone of rechargeable battery technology, it powers innovations in energy storage and electric vehicles, making lithium an indispensable component in the global journey toward a more sustainable, decarbonized future.

Fundamental research on lithium-ion batteries began in the 1970s and has made remarkable strides since the 1980s. The first commercial lithium-ion cell appeared in 1991, marking a rapid transition from laboratory research to industrial production, upending our way of life and immediately being hailed as a revolutionary technology.

Today, lithium-ion batteries power the vast majority of our tools, electronic devices, smartphones, tablets, personal computers, watches, bicycles, scooters, automobiles, industrial vehicles, and machinery across virtually every sector—most everyday items we rely on draw their energy from lithium cells.

Overall, whether viewed through the lens of scientific research or commercial market development, the lithium-ion ecosystem is poised to grow at an annual rate exceeding 30% between 2022 and 2030. By then its value will surpass USD 400 billion and its cumulative market capacity will reach 4.7 TWh.

In electric vehicles, the two principal lithium compounds in use are:

  • Lithium hydroxide (LiOH) for high-end NCM battery series
  • Lithium carbonate (Li₂CO₃) for the most widespread lithium-ion batteries

Lithium in EVs

Global EV Industry

1. Exploding EV Capacity Drives Lithium Demand

Global automakers have announced massive EV production targets by 2030：

  • Tesla: 20 million vehicles; $400 billion investment in batteries
  • Volkswagen: 5 million vehicles; $57 billion investment
  • Toyota: 3.5 million vehicles; $13.6 billion investment

This unprecedented ramp-up in battery installations will drive lithium demand into exponential growth, spurring a global race for new lithium mining, processing and refining.

2. Automakers Become Lithium-Supply Architects

To secure raw materials, OEMs are moving upstream：

  • Tesla signs long-term lithium supply agreements and invests directly in mines
  • Volkswagen forges partnerships to build a local European lithium chain
  • Many brands adopt a “mine-to-battery” vertical integration strategy

By taking control of mining and refining, car companies shift from passive buyers to active builders of the lithium supply chain.

3. Geographies Realign Around EV Demand

Key lithium regions and their evolving roles：

  • Australia: world’s largest lithium producer, largely exporting to China
  • Lithium Triangle (Chile, Argentina, Bolivia): richest reserve base
  • China: only modest mining, but 80 % of global refining capacity

Accelerated investments are reshaping national strategies—from mine expansions in South America to new refining hubs in China, the U.S. and Europe.

Global & Regional Supply Chain

Future Development

Lithium as the “white petroleum” of the EV era will remain at the heart of the global energy transition. With national carbon-neutrality targets tightening and EV adoption climbing, lithium demand is set to double over the next decade.

Resource Diversification

  • Exploration of new sources: salt lakes, shale deposits, even seawater extraction.
  • Investment in more efficient, lower-impact extraction technologies.

Closed-Loop & Recycling

  • Advanced recycling processes to recover lithium from spent batteries.
  • Development of a circular supply chain that reduces reliance on virgin ore.

Strategic Implications

As lithium’s value chain becomes greener and more sophisticated, it will also turn into a geopolitical and technological battleground. Countries and companies that secure both resources and cutting-edge extraction or recycling technologies will gain decisive influence in the new energy era.
